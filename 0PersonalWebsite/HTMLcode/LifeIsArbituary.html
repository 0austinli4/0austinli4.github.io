Life is Arbitrary

This morning, I got up with a group of friends to go into town to find a place to eat brunch. We walked around and found that the store we initially wanted to go to was closed. So, we chose a random direction and walked in it trying to find some random food.

Eventually, we came across this small diner that none of us had recognized before. The food was incredibly good. Pancakes, eggs, waffles, and in massive portions as well (appreciated by the broke side of it). Sweet sickly bacon and sausage. By the random number of chances that the initial store was closed and that I would find a second restaurant, I found an amazing breakfast place.

Now, that isn’t the best example for the topic I’m about to write about, but the theme is close enough: sometimes, life is just arbitrary.

Let’s take interests and passions, for example. I’m passionate about computer science and philosophy. I also really enjoy playing soccer and volleyball. Reasonably, those were my two overarching activities in high school: debate and the robotics code team. But what caused me to enter those activities?

Maybe it was because my older brother also did debate in high school. Maybe it was the random flyer I saw one day advertising a intramural volleyball tournament. Maybe it was that one YouTube video that come across my recommended about an introduction to HTML coding.

Some people would argue that these were all built from structures engrained in our everyday relationships rather than arbitrary events in our daily lives. We are more likely to follow the steps of our siblings because we are directed by their influence. Our town’s structure and booster club fostered an environment motivating more students to try out volleyball. YouTube… has an algorithm.

But that doesn’t change the intention of what I’m trying to get across: how many of our choices are truly determined by ourselves?

We make decisions that we see make sense in the moment. But out of thousands and thousands of these, I would say that 99% of them are random whims or our emotions or the states of affairs on our ongoing lives. The smallest little choices in life, like joining a intramural sports team, can decide your life long friends.

So then, why do people hyper-analyze every decision of their own lives and the decisions in other people’s lives? High school students become depressed when they don’t get into the college of their dreams - yet, when they meet the other amazing people that enter their lives due to whichever location they end up, they seem to forget all about that. That girl that isn’t responding to you for six hours doesn’t actually hate you. There isn’t a concrete point as to why we should strive for such a grasp over our lives, especially when it won’t lead to anything that is especially beneficial in our lives.

Especially, the closest relationships that we build can never be created from a forced type of action. If we didn’t have common classes with other students that we become friends with, how would we have ever met? If we didn’t decide on a whim to start a conversation with someone in the middle of the school cafeteria, would we have found our best friend? What about when a teacher put two people into a group that hated each other, and they eventually started dating? Each of these events seems arbitrary in the moment - and it’s because they are. The first meeting between two strangers never seems like a conscious effort or foot placed forward to put themselves out there, but a matter of pure coincidence.

There just isn't that much meaning in constantly searching for meaning in our lives.
