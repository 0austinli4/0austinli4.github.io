Life is Arbitrary

This morning, I got up with a group of friends to go into town to find a place to eat brunch. We walked around and found that the store we initially wanted to go to was closed on Sundays. Lamentfully, we chose a random direction and walked in it trying to find any food.

Eventually, we came across this small diner that none of us recognized before. The food was incredibly good. Pancakes, eggs, waffles, and in massive portions (appreciated by the broke side of us). Sweet sickly bacon and sausage. By the combination of a random chances that lead us from the first resturant to the second, I found an amazing breafast location.

Now, that isn’t the best example for the topic I want to write about, but the theme is close enough: sometimes, life is just arbitrary.

Let’s take interests and passions, for example. I’m passionate about computer science and philosophy. It makes sense: debate, robotics, combined with volleyball and soccer were the activites that I spent the most time on in high school. But what caused me to start those activities?

Maybe it was because my older brother did debate in high school. Maybe it was that one YouTube video that come across my recommended about an introduction to HTML coding. Maybe it was the random flyer I saw one day advertising a intramural volleyball tournament.

Some would argue that were built from structures engrained in our everyday relationships rather than arbitrary events in our daily lives. We are more likely to follow the steps of our siblings because we are directed by their influence. I started soccer as a 5 year old - out of baseball, basketball, and soccer, it was the only one I kept up into middle school. In other instances, our town’s booster club fostered an environment motivating more students to try out volleyball. YouTube… has an algorithm.

But even in those instances, where all our experiences added up form our interests, the same idea applies: how many of our choices are truly determined by ourselves?

Most of the time, we make decisions that seem to make sense in the moment. But out of thousands and thousands of these, I would say that 99% of them are random whims or our emotions or the states of affairs on our ongoing lives. The smallest little choices in life, like joining a intramural sports team, can decide your life long friends.

So then, why do people hyper-analyze every decision of their own lives and the decisions in other people’s lives? We want to know the meaning of everything - from why that boy or girl typed a word using capitalization or without to why that friend said that particular thing that hurt your feelings that one time. I don't think there's a reason we should look to have absolute control over our lives, especially when it won’t lead to anything beneficial.

Especially, the closest relationships that we build can never be created from a forced type of action. If we didn’t have common classes with other students that we become friends with, how would we have ever met? If we didn’t decide on a whim to start a conversation with someone in the middle of the school cafeteria, would we have found our best friend? What about when a teacher put two people into a group that hated each other, and they eventually started dating? Each of these events seems arbitrary in the moment - and it’s because they are. The first meeting between two strangers never seems like a conscious effort or foot placed forward to put themselves out there, but pure coincidence.

Maybe the same goes for our careers. "What do you want to do when your older" - but is there really such a set path for someone's interests? What we like and don't like aren't defining characteristics of ourselves but arbitrary features of our lives that have been a part of us without a real, conscious choice. Then, in the future, should I just pick my interests at random? Why is it that I should like chewing on ice, but absolute hate the taste of brocolli?

I probably wrote all of this since I have no clue what I want to do when I'm older.
